In <a href="/wiki/Physics" title="Physics">physics</a>, a <b>quantum</b> (plural: <b>quanta</b>) is the minimum amount of any physical entity (<a href="/wiki/Physical_property#List_of_properties" title="Physical property">physical property</a>) involved in an <a href="/wiki/Fundamental_interaction" title="Fundamental interaction">interaction</a>. The fundamental notion that a physical property may be "quantized" is referred to as "the hypothesis of <a href="/wiki/Quantization_(physics)" title="Quantization (physics)">quantization</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> This means that the magnitude of the physical property can take on only discrete values consisting of integer multiples of one quantum.
</p>
For example, a <a href="/wiki/Photon" title="Photon">photon</a> is a single quantum of <a href="/wiki/Light" title="Light">light</a> (or of any other form of <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_radiation" title="Electromagnetic radiation">electromagnetic radiation</a>). Similarly, the energy of an <a href="/wiki/Electron" title="Electron">electron</a> bound within an <a href="/wiki/Atom" title="Atom">atom</a> is quantized and can exist only in certain discrete values. (Indeed, atoms and matter in general are stable because electrons can exist only at discrete energy levels within an atom.) Quantization is one of the foundations of the much broader physics of <a href="/wiki/Quantum_mechanics" title="Quantum mechanics">quantum mechanics</a>. Quantization of energy and its influence on how energy and matter interact (<a href="/wiki/Quantum_electrodynamics" title="Quantum electrodynamics">quantum electrodynamics</a>) is part of the fundamental framework for understanding and describing nature.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology_and_discovery">Etymology and discovery</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Quantum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology and discovery">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

The word <i>quantum</i> comes from the <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> <i>quantus</i>, meaning "how great". "Quanta", short for "quanta of electricity" (electrons), was used in a 1902 article on the photoelectric effect by <a href="/wiki/Philipp_Lenard" title="Philipp Lenard">Philipp Lenard</a>, who credited <a href="/wiki/Hermann_von_Helmholtz" title="Hermann von Helmholtz">Hermann von Helmholtz</a> for using the word in the area of electricity. However, the word <i>quantum</i> in general was well known before 1900.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> It was often used by <a href="/wiki/Physicians" class="mw-redirect" title="Physicians">physicians</a>, such as in the term <i><a href="/wiki/Quantum_satis" title="Quantum satis">quantum satis</a></i>. Both Helmholtz and <a href="/wiki/Julius_von_Mayer" title="Julius von Mayer">Julius von Mayer</a> were physicians as well as physicists. Helmholtz used <i>quantum</i> with reference to heat in his article<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> on Mayer's work, and the word <i>quantum</i> can be found in the formulation of the <a href="/wiki/First_law_of_thermodynamics" title="First law of thermodynamics">first law of thermodynamics</a> by Mayer in his letter<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> dated July 24, 1841. 
</p>
In 1901, <a href="/wiki/Max_Planck" title="Max Planck">Max Planck</a> used <i>quanta</i> to mean "quanta of matter and electricity",<sup id="cite_ref-Planck1901_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Planck1901-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> gas, and heat.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> In 1905, in response to Planck's work and the experimental work of Lenard (who explained his results by using the term <i>quanta of electricity</i>), <a href="/wiki/Albert_Einstein" title="Albert Einstein">Albert Einstein</a> suggested that <a href="/wiki/Radiation" title="Radiation">radiation</a> existed in spatially localized packets which he called <a href="/wiki/Photons" class="mw-redirect" title="Photons">"quanta of light"</a> ("Lichtquanta").<sup id="cite_ref-Einstein1905_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Einstein1905-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
The concept of quantization of radiation was discovered in 1900 by <a href="/wiki/Max_Planck" title="Max Planck">Max Planck</a>, who had been trying to understand the emission of radiation from heated objects, known as <a href="/wiki/Black-body_radiation" title="Black-body radiation">black-body radiation</a>. By assuming that energy can be absorbed or released only in tiny, differential, discrete packets (which he called "bundles", or "energy elements"),<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Planck accounted for certain objects changing colour when heated.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> On December 14, 1900, Planck reported his <a href="/wiki/Planck%27s_law" title="Planck&#39;s law">findings</a> to the <a href="/wiki/German_Physical_Society" title="German Physical Society">German Physical Society</a>, and introduced the idea of quantization for the first time as a part of his research on black-body radiation.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> As a result of his experiments, Planck deduced the numerical value of <i>h</i>, known as the <a href="/wiki/Planck_constant" title="Planck constant">Planck constant</a>, and reported more precise values for the unit of <a href="/wiki/Electrical_charge" class="mw-redirect" title="Electrical charge">electrical charge</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Avogadro%27s_number" class="mw-redirect" title="Avogadro&#39;s number">Avogadro&#8211;Loschmidt number</a>, the number of real molecules in a <a href="/wiki/Mole_(unit)" title="Mole (unit)">mole</a>, to the German Physical Society. After his theory was validated, Planck was awarded the Nobel Prize in Physics for his discovery in 1918.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Beyond_electromagnetic_radiation">Beyond electromagnetic radiation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Quantum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Beyond electromagnetic radiation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

While quantization was first discovered in electromagnetic radiation, it describes a fundamental aspect of energy not just restricted to photons.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
In the attempt to bring theory into agreement with experiment, Max Planck postulated that electromagnetic energy is absorbed or emitted in discrete packets, or quanta.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
